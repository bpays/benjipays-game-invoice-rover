The Problem

Trade shows are expensive.
Forgettable is worse.

Most MSP conference booths look identical. Banner. Demo screen. Awkward conversation. Nobody remembers who they talked to 48 hours later. We needed something that broke through — and that we'd actually own.

01

No natural entry point

Payment automation is hard to demo without a live integration. Most prospects have no visceral sense of what the pain actually feels like. We needed a way to make the problem tangible in 30 seconds.

02

Lead quality at events is low

Badge scans produce a list of names. Nobody opted in, nobody engaged. We wanted leads who self-selected — people who played 3+ rounds and put their real email in to see their name on the board.

03

One-event assets are wasteful

Booths get broken down. Swag gets left behind. Nothing compounds. We wanted to build something that lives at a permanent URL and continues generating leads between events and after social posts.

04

The brand needed a moment

Benji the dog mascot has enormous untapped potential. We've never given him a role. Invoice Rover is the first time Benji does something — and it's on-brand at every level of the game.

The Solution

A game that is also a pipeline.

Invoice Rover is a branded browser-based endless runner starring Benji. Players scan a QR code, enter their name and email to compete, and immediately start playing on their phone. No app. No friction. Name and email go straight to our CRM.

The game is built around our product's core value: Benji dodges bad payments — paper checks, overdue invoices, angry clients — and collects good ones: ACH transfers, instant pay, auto-pay badges. Every mechanic is a product metaphor. Players absorb the brand message without realising they're receiving it.

After the event it stays live at a permanent URL. Every social post sends people to the same game — and each play captures another lead.

The Science

Every design decision
was intentional.

Invoice Rover isn't a game with branding slapped on. The mechanics, progression, and UX are built around specific behavioural goals — engagement duration, replayability, emotional connection, and lead quality.

🧠

Variable reward scheduling

Obstacles and collectibles spawn in semi-random patterns rather than fixed sequences. This is the same principle that makes slot machines compelling — the player never knows exactly what's coming. But skilled players can still read lanes and feel genuinely good at the game.

Psychology · Retention
📈

Difficulty ramp & warm-up zone

The first 30 seconds are deliberately easy — Vancouver is forgiving, speed is low. Players need to survive long enough to feel competent before the game gets hard. Players who feel competent come back. Players who die immediately at a booth demo don't.

Game Feel · Booth Ops
💥

Clutch Save mechanic

The first hit doesn't end the run. It resets the combo and triggers brief invincibility. The game feels fair, not punishing. Players who "almost died" and survived are far more likely to share the experience than players who felt cheated.

Fairness · Shareability
🌆

City progression as storytelling

Ten cities aren't just visual variety — they're a progress map. Players who reach Dallas feel accomplished in a way a score number alone doesn't convey. Cities give players something to report: "I made it to New York." This social currency drives repeat plays and word-of-mouth.

Narrative · Social Currency
🌙

Night mode as emotional spike

Power-ups trigger a live day-to-night city transition with a music energy spike. This sudden environmental shift creates a memorable moment — the kind of thing a player describes to the next person in line. It also resets the player's emotional state, extending average run length.

Emotion · Run Length
🎯

Prize structure as leaderboard engine

Daily resets + a daily prize + a grand prize across 3 days creates two separate competitive incentives. Daily resets mean yesterday's leader starts fresh — keeping Day 2 and 3 players engaged. This is tournament psychology, not raffle psychology.

Competition · Retention
Game Mechanics

Built different.
For a reason.

💰Paid in Full
Collecting this power-up instantly clears every obstacle on screen in a burst of particles. Named after Invoice Rover's core feature. Auto-triggers — no button, no delay. Every bad payment: resolved.
Product metaphor
🛡️Auto-Pay Shield
15 seconds of full invincibility. Style shifts to aggressive collecting. Named after the hands-off automation promise of Auto-Pay. Night mode activates. Nothing can touch you.
Product metaphor
⚡Instant Pay Boost
Speed +60%, score ×2. High risk, high reward. The lanes feel tight, obstacles come fast. Exactly how instant payment feels — exhilarating and slightly out of control. 12 seconds.
Brand energy
💫Payment Streak
Double points. Every collect adds 0.5 seconds to the timer. Skilled players extend well past 15 seconds. The mechanic is compound interest made playable.
Compound interest = our pitch
🤸Clutch Save
First hit of any run doesn't end the game. Resets combo + brief invincibility flash. Second hit ends it. Players who "should have died" and survived are far more likely to come back.
Fairness = replayability
⏱️Delta-Time Normalization
Game speed is tied to real time, not frame rate. Identical experience at 30fps on a cheap Android and 120fps on a MacBook. Critical for a conference where attendees have wildly different devices.
Technical fairness
